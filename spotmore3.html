Chocolate Hills (Philippines)

The Chocolate Hills are a unique geological formation located in the province of Bohol in the central Philippines. These hills are one of the most famous natural landmarks in the country and are a UNESCO World Heritage Site nominee due to their distinct appearance and historical significance. They are primarily known for their almost perfectly symmetrical, cone-shaped hills, which, during the dry season, turn a rich brown color, giving rise to their name.

Number and Size: The Chocolate Hills consist of about 1,268 to 1,776 hills, spread over an area of approximately 50 square kilometers (19 square miles) in the towns of Carmen, Batuan, and Sagbayan. These hills vary in size, ranging from as low as 30 meters (98 feet) to as high as 120 meters (394 feet). Though they appear to be separate, the hills are actually made up of limestone and are the result of a combination of geological processes that occurred millions of years ago.

Formation Process: The origin of the Chocolate Hills dates back to around 2 to 5 million years ago, when the area was under a shallow sea. The coral reefs formed over time, and as the sea level receded, the reefs were lifted above sea level and hardened into limestone. Erosion then sculpted the soft, underlying rock, creating the hill formations we see today. The unique, conical shape of the hills is a result of the constant erosion and weathering of the limestone over millennia.

Color Change: The name "Chocolate Hills" comes from the hills' appearance during the dry season, typically from March to May, when the grass covering the hills turns brown, giving them a chocolate-like appearance. During the wet season, the hills are covered with green grass, making them appear more vibrant and lush.
